Dosage

ORAL › 50 mg tablet (Rx)

Acute treatment of migraine, by mouth, ADULT >18 years, initially a single dose 50 mg tablet; if the migraine has not
resolved by 2 hours after administration, or returns after a transient improvement, administer a second dose at
least 2 hours after the first dose (maximum daily dose, 200 mg in a 24-hour period).

Administration

ORAL › 50 mg tablet (Rx)

Administer as soon as symptoms appear.

Dose Adjustment

Geriatric:
Administer first dose at physician’s office, if needed.

Hepatic Impairment:
Reduce doses, in mild-to-moderate hepatic impairment, the maximum single dose should not exceed 50 mg.

Indications

Acute treatment of migraine with or without aura.

NOTE: NOT for the prevention of migraine attacks

Contraindications

Hypersensitivity to sumatriptan or any component of the formulation; Severe hepatic impairment; ischemic coronary
artery disease or coronary artery vasospasm, including Prinzmetal’s angina; uncontrolled hypertension; Wolff-
Parkinson-White syndrome or arrhythmias associated with other cardiac accessory conduction pathway disorders;
history of stroke or transient ischemic attack; history of hemiplegic or basilar migraine; peripheral vascular disease;
ischemic bowel disease; recent use (within 24 hours) of ergotamine-containing medication or another 5-HT1 agonist;
concurrent or recent (within 2 weeks) use of a MAO-A inhibitor.

Precautions

Cardiac events (e.g., coronary artery vasospasm, transient ischemia, MI, ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation, cardiac
arrest, and death); arrhythmias (lifethreatening); elevated blood pressure (hypertensive crisis) Cerebrovascular
events (e.g., cerebral hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage, and stroke; some fatal); vasospasm reactions; CNS
depression; seizures; ocular effects.

Serotonin syndrome; anaphylactic or anaphylactoid reactions (may be fatal); hepatic impairment.

Elderly (may be at increased cardiovascular risk); children (safety and effectiveness have not been established).

Pregnancy; (has been associated with embryolethality, fetal abnormalities, and pup mortality in animal studies);
lactation (excreted in breastmilk; use with caution).

SKILLED TASKS. May impair ability to perform tasks, which require mental alertness, like driving or operating
machinery.

Adverse Drug Reactions

Common: Hot and cold flashes, chest pain, chest pressure, chest tightness, pain, sensation of pressure, paresthesia,
malaise, fatigue, feeling of heaviness, sensation of tightness, heaviness of chest, sore throat, jaw pain, jaw
pressure, jaw tightness, pharyngeal edema, neck pain, local pain

Less Common: Hypotension, palpitations, dystonia, tremor, vertigo

Drug Interactions

Monitor closely with:
Enhances therapeutic effect of Sumatriptan: Antipsychotic Agents (serotonergic effect)
Increases risk of adverse or toxic effects of Sumatriptan: Antipsychotic Agents (enhances dopamine blockade,
possibly increasing the risk for neuroleptic malignant syndrome; serotonin syndrome) Metoclopramide (serotonin
syndrome; neuroleptic malignant syndrome)

Avoid concomitant use with:
Enhances therapeutic effect of Sumatriptan: Beta Blockers Ergot Derivatives [except Nicergoline] (vasoconstricting
effect) Metaxalone, Methylene blue, Methylphenidate, Opioid Analgesics, Tramadol (serotonergic effect) Other
Serotonin 5HT1D Receptor Agonists
Enhances therapeutic effect of the following drugs: Ergot Derivatives [except Nicergoline], Serotonin 5HT1D
Receptor Agonists (vasoconstricting effect)
Increases risk of adverse or toxic effects of Sumatriptan: Other Serotonin Modulators [except Tedizolid] Metaxalone,
Methylene blue (serotonin syndrome) Methylphenidate (serotonin toxicity) Opioid Analgesics, Tramadol
Increases risk of adverse or toxic effects of the following drugs: Dapoxetine Tramadol (seizures)
Increases serum concentration of Sumatriptan: MAO Inhibitors

Mechanism of Action

A selective serotonin agonist that acts at 5-HT1B/1D receptor, causing vasoconstriction of meningeal vessels and/or
inhibition of neurogenic inflammatory processes in the CNS. Its nociceptive effect is likely due to blocking the pain
transmission signals at trigeminal nerve terminals and synapses of second-order neurons in trigeminal nucleus
caudalis.
